# ⚖️ Professional Values {#professional-values}  

Through my previous roles I learned values beyond the code, values that help the team from conception to release. These are the values I bring to your team.  

## 🤝 Transparency and communication  
This is my top priority. I make sure the team knows when a task will be done, how we plan to develop it, and why we approach it that way.  

If we can't deliver on time, the next step is to discuss openly what can be done within the time we have. Delaying bad news, keeping a task "almost done" for weeks, or omitting the release date are just ways of avoiding accountability. I push for transparency in these cases, because early clarity gives the team room to react.  

## ⏳ Process  
Inherently every development team has a process, even if it is 'Go Horse,' it is still a process. It is in my nature to understand how the process actually works, document it clearly, and improve it step by step. This creates predictability and helps the team deliver faster over time.  

## 🤩 User impact first  
Frontend codebases often "die" in 5 years (rewrites, framework shifts, or total redesigns). Backends last a bit longer, maybe 10 years, before major replacement or replatforming. On the other hand, a lost user is a forever lost user. New features, analytics, redesigns, and refactorings mean nothing if the user is gone. Whatever I build, the priority is always what is best for the final user.  

## 🏠 Ownership mindset  
I treat the software as if I were the owner. That means I care about quality, stability, and user experience, not just moving tasks to 'done'. After years of handling bad code, I feel responsible for long-term maintainability and for always leaving the codebase better than I found it.  

## 🍎 Healthy code  
If you don't take your time to maintain the code, the code will take your time from you. Acknowledging or not, maintenance is required. I delay it by writing clean, structured code from the start. When that is not possible, I focus on refactoring bad patterns, improving readability, and simplifying structures. Sometimes a codebase needs significant maintenance, and the only safe way is to get the team to acknowledge it and schedule time for it.  

## 🏰 Resilient code  
Healthy code doesn't mean it is resilient. A codebase can be clean and organized, but still collapses at runtime, with unexpected cases or heavy load. A mindful developer accounts not only for the code, but also for the hardware, the environment, the data flow, and how systems behave under pressure. This is why I always code for the worst case, catching exceptions, validating input data, creating fallbacks instead of assuming the happy path, and logging external processes.
